# On Learning to Code

I still remember the first time a `for` loop made sense. Not just the syntax — I mean *really* clicked. The moment when the abstraction dissolved and I could feel the machine iterating.

That feeling is addictive. It's also rare.

## What No One Tells You

Most tutorials show you the clean path. Input → understanding → output. What they don't show is the messy middle:

- Staring at an error message for three hours only to find a missing semicolon
- Feeling like everyone else gets it except you
- Building something that works, not knowing *why* it works
- Coming back to code you wrote six months ago and not recognizing it

This is normal. This is all of us.

## The Identity Shift

Learning to code isn't just a skill acquisition — it's an identity shift. You're training yourself to think in systems, to hold abstractions in your head, to embrace uncertainty.

The people who stick with it aren't necessarily smarter. They've learned to sit with confusion.

## What Actually Helps

**Build something you care about.** Tutorials get you started but projects keep you going. Even a tiny tool that solves *your* problem.

**Read other people's code.** After a year of writing, I spent six months reading open source code. It changed how I wrote.

**Embrace the docs.** Google is for finding the docs. The docs are for understanding the answer.

**Slow down to go fast.** The urge to copy-paste solutions is strong. Resist it. Type the code yourself. Make the error. Fix it.

## A Note on Comparison

Comparison is the thief of learning. Someone always seems further ahead. They might have three years more experience, a CS degree, or six hours a day to study.

Your path is your path. Measure against yesterday, not against others.

---

Twelve years in, I'm still learning. The stack changes, the frameworks change, the problems change. The discomfort of not knowing never fully goes away — it just becomes more familiar.

And somehow, that feels like the point.
